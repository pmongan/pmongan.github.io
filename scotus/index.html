The Cases
That Shape Social Work Practice

Supreme Court decisions every social worker should know.

About This Series

Most social work students will never read a Supreme Court opinion. They will, however, work inside the systems those opinions built. Child welfare, civil commitment, special education, public benefits, and mental health all operate under rules created by the cases on this page. This series translates those cases into plain language, so you can recognize them when they show up in your work. If you're unfamiliar with how the Supreme Court works, start with Understanding How SCOTUS Works before going through the cases.

How to Use This Page

Cases are grouped by practice area. Each entry shows the case name, citation, vote, a one-sentence holding, and what it changes about your daily work. Click any case name or the Read button to open the full explainer. The honorable mentions at the bottom round out the list with cases worth knowing about, even without a full treatment.

Practice Area01

Confidentiality & Privilege

The legal protection of what a client tells you in a counseling session. This is the doctrinal foundation of clinical practice and the single most important Supreme Court decision for clinical social workers.

01

Jaffee v. Redmond

518 U.S. 1 (1996) · Vote: 7–2

Confidential communications between a licensed psychotherapist, explicitly including a licensed clinical social worker, and a client are privileged from compelled disclosure in federal court.

Why it matters

Your therapy notes and session content are presumptively protected. Anchors NASW Code of Ethics §1.07 in evidentiary law. Shapes how you document, respond to subpoenas, and explain the limits of confidentiality to clients.

Practice Area02

Child Welfare & Family Integrity

CPS, foster care, termination of parental rights, parental autonomy, and tribal sovereignty. These four cases form the constitutional architecture of every child welfare proceeding in the country.

02

DeShaney v. Winnebago County DSS

489 U.S. 189 (1989) · Vote: 6–3

The Constitution does not impose an affirmative duty on the state to protect a child from harm by a private actor, even when caseworkers know about the abuse and fail to intervene.

Why it matters

Sets the rule that federal §1983 claims against CPS for failing to act almost always fail. Defines the "special relationship" doctrine that does apply once a child is in state custody. Famous for Blackmun's "Poor Joshua!" dissent.

Read →
03

Santosky v. Kramer

455 U.S. 745 (1982) · Vote: 5–4

Before a state may permanently terminate parental rights, it must prove its grounds by at least clear and convincing evidence. Preponderance is constitutionally insufficient.

Why it matters

Sets the constitutional floor for the burden of proof in every TPR case. Your documentation, case notes, and testimony must be capable of supporting that elevated standard. ICWA cases require beyond a reasonable doubt, which is even higher.

Read →
04

Troxel v. Granville

530 U.S. 57 (2000) · Vote: 6–3

A fit parent's decision about who has contact with their child is presumed correct, and courts cannot override it through a simple "best interests of the child" balancing test.

Why it matters

Operationalizes the "fit parent presumption" in family law. Until a parent has been adjudicated unfit, courts assume their decisions about their child are correct. Constrains how casework recommendations can be framed in intact families.

Read →
05

Haaland v. Brackeen

599 U.S. 255 (2023) · Vote: 7–2

The Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA) is constitutional. Tribal placement preferences, "active efforts," and the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt TPR standard for Indian children remain federal law.

Why it matters

Screen every child welfare case for ICWA applicability from first contact. Provide notice to tribes. Honor jurisdictional transfer rights. ICWA is called the "gold standard" of child welfare practice for good reason.

Practice Area03

Mental Health & Disability Rights

Civil commitment, deinstitutionalization, community integration. These three cases together set the constitutional rules for when the state can confine someone with mental illness, what it must prove, and what it owes them when they're ready to come out.

06

O'Connor v. Donaldson

422 U.S. 563 (1975) · Vote: 9–0

A state cannot confine a non-dangerous person who can survive safely in the community in a psychiatric institution against their will. A mental illness diagnosis alone is not enough.

Why it matters

Established dangerousness (or grave disability) as a constitutional prerequisite for continued involuntary commitment. The legal foundation of deinstitutionalization and modern least-restrictive-alternative analysis.

Read →
07

Addington v. Texas

441 U.S. 418 (1979) · Vote: 8–0

Due process requires the state to prove the grounds for involuntary civil commitment by at least clear and convincing evidence. Preponderance is too low; reasonable doubt is too high.

Why it matters

Sets the evidentiary standard for every involuntary commitment hearing. Your assessments and testimony must support this elevated burden. The same standard applies in TPR and many guardianships.

Read →
08

Olmstead v. L.C.

527 U.S. 581 (1999) · Vote: 6–3

Under the ADA, states must provide community-based services for people with disabilities when treatment professionals deem it appropriate, the person doesn't oppose it, and it can be reasonably accommodated.

Why it matters

The most important disability-rights case for social work. Discharge planning from psychiatric hospitals, nursing facilities, and state institutions must consider the most integrated setting. Drives Money Follows the Person, HCBS waivers, and state Olmstead plans.

Practice Area04

Schools & Special Education

For school social workers, behavioral health clinicians, and foster care workers managing educational stability. This case sets the floor every Individualized Education Program must clear.

09

Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District

580 U.S. 386 (2017) · Vote: 8–0

A school's IEP must be "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances." Merely more than trivial progress is not enough.

Why it matters

Raises the IDEA floor. IEPs must be "appropriately ambitious." When you sit in IEP meetings or write social/developmental histories, schools must offer a cogent explanation for their IEP choices.

Practice Area05

Public Benefits & Due Process

When can the government cut off a client's TANF, SNAP, Medicaid, or SSI? Not without notice and a hearing. This foundational case is the doctrinal anchor of every benefits termination procedure your clients will face.

10

Goldberg v. Kelly

397 U.S. 254 (1970) · Vote: 5–3

Welfare benefits are a statutory entitlement protected by the Due Process Clause. Recipients are entitled to a pre-termination evidentiary hearing with notice, the right to appear, and continued benefits while they appeal.

Why it matters

Foundational for public-benefits practice. Goldberg-style procedures appear in nearly every means-tested program. Equips you to recognize when an agency is short-circuiting due process and to refer clients for fair-hearing representation.

Also Worth Knowing+

Honorable Mentions

Cases that didn't make the top ten but deserve recognition. Each is still good law. These are most relevant to specific practice settings. Where a full explainer exists, the case name links to it.

Civil Commitment & Institutional Rights
  • Parham v. J.R.
    442 U.S. 584 (1979) · 6–3

    Parents may "voluntarily" commit a minor to a psychiatric facility with only a neutral physician's confirmation. No adversary hearing required. Crucial for adolescent inpatient social work.

  • Youngberg v. Romeo
    457 U.S. 307 (1982) · 9–0

    Persons involuntarily committed to state institutions have constitutional rights to safe conditions, freedom from undue restraint, and minimally adequate training. Essential for ID/DD social work.

Parental Rights & Family Integrity
  • Stanley v. Illinois
    405 U.S. 645 (1972) · 5–2

    An unwed biological father is entitled to a hearing on his fitness before the state can take his children. Foundation of putative-father rights. Pairs with Santosky and Troxel.

  • Pierce v. Society of Sisters
    268 U.S. 510 (1925) · 9–0

    Historical anchor of parents' rights to direct the upbringing and education of their children. Foundational substantive due-process case underlying Troxel.

  • Wisconsin v. Yoder
    406 U.S. 205 (1972) · 6–1

    Amish parents' free-exercise rights allowed exemption from compulsory schooling beyond eighth grade. Companion case to Pierce on parental autonomy in education.

  • Adoptive Couple v. Baby Girl
    570 U.S. 637 (2013) · 5–4

    Narrows ICWA's TPR provisions in a specific factual posture involving a noncustodial biological father. Pairs with Brackeen.

Benefits & Procedural Due Process
  • Mathews v. Eldridge
    424 U.S. 319 (1976) · 6–2

    Three-factor due-process balancing test that governs most benefits-termination cases today. Explains why Social Security disability uses lighter procedures than Goldberg required for welfare.

Special Education
  • Board of Education v. Rowley
    458 U.S. 176 (1982) · 6–3

    The original IDEA / FAPE case. Now read through the Endrew F. lens but still good law. Worth knowing as the doctrinal foundation Endrew F. built on.

Immigrant Clients & School Social Work
  • Plyler v. Doe
    457 U.S. 202 (1982) · 5–4

    States may not deny free public K–12 education to undocumented children. Important for school social workers and any worker serving immigrant families.

LGBTQ+ Clients
  • Obergefell v. Hodges
    576 U.S. 644 (2015) · 5–4

    Marriage equality nationwide. Affects family-formation work, foster and adoptive licensing, and second-parent adoptions.

  • Bostock v. Clayton County
    590 U.S. 644 (2020) · 6–3

    Title VII's ban on sex discrimination protects gay, lesbian, and transgender employees. Relevant to workplace issues for LGBTQ+ clients.

  • Chiles v. Salazar
    606 U.S. ___ (2026) · 8–1

    Colorado's conversion therapy ban, as applied to talk therapy with minors, regulates speech by viewpoint and must survive strict scrutiny. Case remanded; statute not yet struck down.

Juvenile Justice
  • Roper v. Simmons
    543 U.S. 551 (2005) · 5–4

    Eighth Amendment bars the death penalty for offenses committed under age 18. Foundational for adolescent-brain-development arguments in social work testimony.

  • Graham v. Florida
    560 U.S. 48 (2010) · 6–3

    Life without parole is unconstitutional for juveniles convicted of non-homicide offenses. Part of the juvenile-sentencing trilogy with Roper and Miller.

  • Miller v. Alabama
    567 U.S. 460 (2012) · 5–4

    Mandatory life without parole for juvenile homicide offenders is unconstitutional. Requires individualized sentencing considering youth. Made retroactive by Montgomery (2016).

Intellectual Disability & Sentencing
  • Atkins v. Virginia
    536 U.S. 304 (2002) · 6–3

    Executing a person with intellectual disability violates the Eighth Amendment. Relevant when social workers conduct adaptive-functioning assessments in capital-mitigation work.

  • Hall v. Florida
    572 U.S. 701 (2014) · 5–4

    States cannot use a strict IQ-score cutoff to deny an Atkins claim. Standard error of measurement must be considered.
